If the lesson here is that few people use proxies, we should remove the feature as bloat. If the lesson here is that our proxy support isn't good enough, then we should still remove the feature and replace it with something better. 3817 reads: "I thought Transmission's proxy support was half-assed, and didn't think many people used it. The conclusion was to remove the feature to see if anyone noticed, as a way to measuring how important the feature was to users, and whether peer-based support should be added."

ijuxda wrote:2. Why was a another ticket started (#3817) when the feedback in #3688 was clearly not in favor of the changes?

The reasons for opening #3817 were described exactly where you would expect, in the description to #3817. Maybe I should have left 3817 in 3688 but that is a bookkeeping issue.

  1. Regarding the commits themselves: r11512 was pushed when the libtransmission and GTK+ code was done; r11514 was pushed when the Qt code was done, and the other two commits were documentation improvements suggested by a user. This is pretty straightforward.
  2. Regarding the ticket being open: livings hasn't finished the Mac client code yet, so the ticket is still open.
  3. Regarding why I landed the code despite lack of agreement: as I've said, I thought the ticket was an all-around win. And I was right: most complaints stopped when users saw what Desktop Proxy Integration looked like. Also, where is it written that I can't commit code if you disagree with it? I make no demands or hold any expectations, but I would like to make a public personal conduct request to Jordan regarding any future disagreements with other people:

  1. Try to remain calm and polite even if you read part of something that upsets you. Take a moment to clear your head and realize you have enough time to form a reply to the best of your ability.
  2. Don't feel you need to reply to every single thing being said; reflect on the argument as a whole and choose the most salient aspect for refutation.   4. Be careful when interpreting implications, vagueness, sarcasm or the like. All statements are imprecise to some degree, so one person's assigned meaning will generally not match another's, nor may it match the author's intent.
I know it's naive to assume that the lead programmer, server owner and forum administrator should be bound by anything I have to say, but I wanted to provide these suggestions in the interests of improving collaboration should a similar situation ever arise in the future.
